How to File an Insurance Claim After a Car Accident (Step-by-Step)

Published April 6, 2026 | Updated April 6, 2026 | By ClaimCheck

A car accident is stressful enough without navigating the insurance claim process. This comprehensive guide walks you through every step, from the immediate aftermath to receiving your settlement. Whether you're filing your first claim or returning as a repeat policyholder, these steps will help you maximize your compensation and avoid costly mistakes.

Step 1: Ensure Safety and Call for Help (First 30 Minutes)

Your first priority after a car accident is the safety of everyone involved. Before worrying about your insurance claim, follow these critical initial steps.

Immediate Actions

  1. Check for injuries: Ask all passengers and the other driver if anyone is injured. If yes, call 911 immediately. Do not move anyone who appears seriously hurt.
  2. Move vehicles to safety: If the accident is minor and your vehicle is drivable, move it to the shoulder or a safe location away from traffic. Turn on hazard lights.
  3. Call police: For any accident with injuries or significant damage, call the police. In most states, you're legally required to report accidents causing injury or property damage over a certain threshold (typically $500-$1000). Get the police report number.
  4. Turn on hazard lights: Warn other drivers by activating your emergency flashing lights.
  5. Set up warning triangles: If safe to do so, place warning triangles or flares 100 feet behind your vehicle on highways.
Important: Never admit fault at the accident scene. Stick to factual statements like "My vehicle was hit" rather than "It was my fault" or "I didn't see them coming." Fault determination is the insurance company's job.

Step 2: Document Everything at the Scene (30 Minutes to 1 Hour)

The information you gather now becomes critical evidence for your claim. Take photos, notes, and contact details while everything is fresh.

Documentation Checklist

  • Photos of all vehicle damage (multiple angles, close-ups)
  • Photos of the accident scene (road conditions, traffic signs, skid marks)
  • Photos of the other driver's license and insurance card
  • Names, phone numbers, and addresses of all involved parties
  • Names and contact info of any witnesses
  • Police report number and officer's name
  • Date, time, and weather conditions
  • Your odometer reading and vehicle mileage
  • The other driver's vehicle make, model, color, and license plate
  • Their insurance company name and policy number

Modern smartphones make documentation easy. Take 20-30 photos covering the damage from all angles, the road surface, traffic signals, and surrounding area. These photos prevent disputes later about the accident's severity or cause.

Step 3: File Your Claim Within the Deadline

Insurance policies include claim filing deadlines. Most require notification within 30 days, though some specify 60 or 90 days. File as soon as possible, ideally within 24-48 hours while details are fresh.

How to File Your Claim

Online filing: Most insurers allow you to file through their website or mobile app. This is the fastest method and creates a timestamped record.

By phone: Call your insurer's claims line (find the number on your insurance card). Have your policy number, the other driver's information, and photos ready. The adjuster will ask detailed questions about the accident.

In person: Visit a local agent office to file. Bring all documentation.

When filing, provide factual, detailed information about what happened. Describe the accident sequence, road conditions, traffic signals, and any contributing factors like weather or vehicle malfunctions. Be consistent—your first statement becomes your official account.

Day 0-1: File Your Claim

Contact your insurer within 24 hours. Provide police report number, photos, and a written account of the accident.

Day 1-3: Adjuster Assignment

Your insurance company assigns a claims adjuster. They'll request additional information and may schedule a vehicle inspection.

Day 3-7: Vehicle Inspection

An appraiser inspects your vehicle to assess damage and estimate repair costs.

Day 7-14: Damage Assessment

The adjuster reviews the appraisal, compares it to the at-fault driver's insurance claim, and determines liability.

Step 4: Gather Supporting Documentation

After filing, your insurer will request documents proving the accident's impact on you. Prepare these materials to support your claim.

Required Documents

  • Police report: Obtain an official copy from the police department that responded
  • Medical records: If injured, provide treatment records and medical bills
  • Repair estimates: Get quotes from 2-3 repair shops
  • Rental car receipts: Keep copies if you rented a vehicle while yours was repaired
  • Proof of ownership: Vehicle registration and title
  • Maintenance records: Show your vehicle was well-maintained (strengthens your position)
  • Proof of coverage: Your active insurance policy at accident time
  • Photos of injury: If injured, take photos of visible injuries (bruises, cuts, etc.)
  • Lost wage documentation: Pay stubs showing income lost due to the accident

Step 5: Understand Liability and Fault Determination

Most car accidents involve clear fault determination, but some are more complex. Your insurance company must determine who was legally responsible.

Common Fault Scenarios

Rear-end collisions: The rear driver is almost always at fault. They have a duty to maintain safe distance and control.

Left-turn accidents: The turning driver is usually at fault for failing to yield to oncoming traffic.

Intersection collisions: The driver who violated the traffic signal, stop sign, or right-of-way is at fault. Police reports and traffic camera footage are critical here.

Lane-change accidents: The driver who changed lanes is typically at fault for failing to check blind spots.

Parking lot accidents: Usually the moving vehicle is at fault. Stationary vehicles bear no responsibility.

Your insurance company investigates fault using police reports, witness statements, vehicle damage patterns, and accident reconstruction if necessary. In some states, fault can be shared (comparative negligence). You might be 10% at fault and the other driver 90% at fault, reducing your compensation by 10%.

Step 6: Negotiate Your Settlement

Once the adjuster completes their investigation, they'll offer a settlement. This isn't always final. You have the right to negotiate.

Settlement Options

  1. Accept the offer: If the amount is fair and covers all your damages, accept. You'll sign a release form.
  2. Negotiate: If you disagree with the amount, request an itemized breakdown of how they calculated the settlement. Provide documentation supporting your position (repair quotes, medical bills, etc.).
  3. Request appraisal: Most policies include an appraisal clause. If you and the insurer disagree on vehicle damage valuation, either party can request an independent appraisal. You each choose an appraiser, who select an umpire. This process costs $500-$800 but is binding.
  4. File a complaint: If the insurer denies your legitimate claim, file a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance. This often prompts reconsideration.

Settlement typically includes vehicle repair costs (or actual cash value if totaled), rental car expenses, and sometimes diminished value (your car's reduced resale value due to accident history). If the other driver was at fault, their insurance pays. If you were at fault, your own collision/comprehensive coverage pays (minus your deductible).

Critical Deadlines and Statutes of Limitations

Claim Filing Deadlines

  • Notice requirement: Most policies require notification within 30 days. Failing to notify voids coverage.
  • Proof of loss: You typically have 30-90 days after the accident to provide proof of damages.
  • Lawsuit statute of limitations: If you plan to sue, you usually have 3-4 years from the accident date (varies by state).
  • Personal injury claims: Medical treatment must begin within a reasonable time of the accident to prove causation. Medical-only claims often have shorter limits.
  • Third-party claims: If the other driver was at fault, their insurer typically has 30-90 days to respond to your claim.

Never miss these deadlines. A single day late can void your right to compensation. Mark dates on your calendar and send correspondence via certified mail for documentation.

Mistakes That Kill Your Claim

Avoid These Common Errors

  • Admitting fault verbally: Statements like "It was my fault" are used against you. Stick to facts.
  • Giving a recorded statement without review: You can and should have an attorney review your statement before providing it.
  • Settling too quickly: Some injuries appear days or weeks later. Don't settle immediately if injured.
  • Failing to report promptly: Delays raise red flags about the accident's legitimacy or severity.
  • Not documenting medical treatment: Get written medical records from every provider. Verbal claims carry little weight.
  • Discussing the accident on social media: Posts and comments can be used as evidence. Stay silent publicly.
  • Losing documentation: Keep all photos, receipts, medical records, and correspondence. Organize them by date.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a car accident claim take?

Simple claims with clear liability resolve in 5-14 days. Complex claims with multiple vehicles, injuries, or liability disputes can take 30-60 days or longer.

What if the other driver doesn't have insurance?

Your uninsured motorist coverage (UM) protects you. File a claim under your own policy. You'll pay your deductible, but UM covers damages the uninsured driver would have paid.

Can I choose my own repair shop?

Yes. Your policy guarantees you the right to choose any qualified repair facility. The insurer cannot force you to use their preferred shop, though they may negotiate the final repair estimate.

What if my car is totaled?

Your insurer will pay the actual cash value (ACV) of your vehicle at the time of loss, minus your deductible. ACV is typically determined by market value guides like NADA and Kelley Blue Book. You can dispute this valuation and request an independent appraisal.

Should I hire a lawyer?

For minor accidents, probably not. For serious injuries, permanent damage, or disputed liability, an attorney protects your interests, often recovering more than their fee costs.
