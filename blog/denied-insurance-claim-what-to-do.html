Denied Insurance Claim? Here's What to Do Next

Published April 6, 2026 | Updated April 6, 2026 | By ClaimCheck

A claim denial is devastating when you're counting on that payout. But denial doesn't mean the end of the road. Most claims can be appealed successfully with the right documentation and strategy. This guide walks you through why claims get denied, how to appeal, what evidence to submit, and how to escalate if necessary.

Why Insurance Claims Get Denied

Understanding the reason for denial is your first step to overturning it. Here are the most common reasons:

1. Policy Exclusions or Limitations

Your policy may not cover the loss. Review your policy documents. Some claims fall outside coverage due to exclusions (intentional damage, acts of war, business use) or limitations (caps on specific losses).

Challenge strategy: If the exclusion is ambiguous, argue it should be interpreted in your favor. Many states require clear, conspicuous language for exclusions to be valid.

2. Lack of Proof of Loss

You failed to provide required documentation within the deadline. Policies require proof of loss within 30-90 days, often with specific forms completed.

Challenge strategy: Submit missing documentation immediately. Many insurers will reconsider if you respond promptly, especially if the deadline hasn't passed yet.

3. Non-Coverage Losses

The loss doesn't fall under your coverage type. For example, homeowner's insurance doesn't cover floods. Flood damage requires separate flood insurance.

Challenge strategy: Verify the cause of loss. If a pipe burst caused water damage (covered), you should recover even if the backup (uncovered) contributed. Partial causation may entitle you to partial recovery.

4. Pre-Existing Condition or Wear and Tear

Insurance covers sudden, accidental damage, not deterioration from age, normal wear, or lack of maintenance.

Challenge strategy: Show the damage was sudden and unexpected, not gradual. Provide evidence of recent maintenance if applicable. Get an independent inspection.

5. Misrepresentation on Your Application

You provided false or incomplete information when applying (occupancy status, prior losses, business operations). Insurers use this to rescind (cancel retroactively) your policy.

Challenge strategy: This is the hardest denial to overturn. Show any misstatement was unintentional. Note that many states require rescission within 2 years. If rescission is denied, consult an attorney.

6. Policy Lapsed Before Loss

Your coverage wasn't active when the loss occurred. Payment was missed or policy was cancelled.

Challenge strategy: If you paid before the loss date, demand reinstatement. If you missed payment, ask about grace periods (many are 30 days). Some policies auto-renew.

7. Fraud or Misstatement in Claim

You're suspected of exaggerating, fabricating, or misrepresenting the claim itself.

Challenge strategy: Respond with detailed, truthful documentation. Fraud claims are serious. If accused, consult an attorney before responding further.

Your Immediate Action Plan (First 48 Hours)

Step 1: Understand the Denial

Read the denial letter carefully. It must provide specific reasons and cite policy sections. If vague (just says "denied"), that's grounds for appeal itself. Request clarification on the exact policy language used to deny the claim.

Step 2: Note the Deadline

Your state law provides an appeal window (usually 30-60 days). Mark this date immediately. Missing the deadline is fatal to your appeal. Set a calendar reminder for 5 days before expiration.

Step 3: Gather Original Documentation

Collect everything: policy documents, original application, claim forms you submitted, photos of the loss, repair estimates, communications with the insurer, and receipts for damaged items.

Step 4: Identify New Evidence

What documentation do you NOT have? Get it now. Order police reports, medical records, professional inspections, repair quotes, or expert evaluations. This new evidence is your leverage.

Step-by-Step Appeal Process

Day 1-2: File Your Appeal

Contact your insurer in writing (certified mail, email with read receipt). State clearly: "I am appealing the denial of claim #[claim number]." Explain why you believe the denial was wrong. Cite specific policy language that supports your position. Submit new documentation.

Day 3-7: Provide Supporting Evidence

Send all documentation supporting your claim. Include professional inspections, repair estimates, photos, expert opinions, and legal arguments. Organize materials chronologically. Include a cover letter summarizing the evidence.

Day 7-21: Await Internal Review

The insurer conducts an internal appeal review (different adjuster than the original decision). This typically takes 14-30 days but must be completed within your state's timeline (usually 60 days from appeal filing).

Day 21-30: Response from Insurer

Insurer responds with reversal, partial reversal, or upheld denial. If reversed, you're paid. If upheld, proceed to the next step.

Day 30-45: File Complaint with Insurance Department

If appeal fails, contact your state's Insurance Commissioner or Department of Insurance. File a formal complaint. Most states investigate at no cost to you. This often prompts insurer reconsideration.

Building Your Appeal: Evidence That Works

Documentation That Strengthens Appeals

  • Professional inspection reports (fire investigator, structural engineer, medical doctor)
  • Repair estimates from licensed contractors (multiple quotes show market value)
  • Receipts and proof of purchase (original cost substantiation)
  • Photos showing the loss and damage details
  • Medical records and bills (proving injury causation)
  • Police reports confirming the incident
  • Expert affidavits (engineer, doctor, specialist opinions)
  • Prior similar claims (showing this is covered loss, not new exclusion)
  • Industry standards (what proper coverage should include)
  • Legal research (case law from your state supporting your position)

How to Write a Strong Appeal Letter

  1. State the facts: When did the loss occur? What happened? Provide a clear, chronological narrative. Stick to facts, not emotions.
  2. Cite policy language: Quote the exact policy section that covers your loss. Explain why denial conflicts with that language.
  3. Address the denial reason: If they denied due to "wear and tear," explain why this was sudden damage, not gradual. If they claimed misrepresentation, explain the unintentional nature.
  4. Provide new evidence: Reference each attached document. Example: "Attached inspection report from [Expert Name] confirms this damage is consistent with sudden loss covered under Section 4(b)."
  5. Legal argument: Briefly explain applicable law. Example: "Under [State] law, ambiguous policy language is interpreted in the insured's favor. The term 'water damage' includes pipe bursts under [Case Citation]."
  6. Demand action: End clearly: "I demand reconsideration of this claim and full payment of $[amount] within 30 days."
Important: Keep appeal letters professional and fact-based. Avoid emotional language, accusations of fraud, or threats. Focus on policy language and evidence. Angry appeals are more likely to fail.

External Review and Government Complaint

If internal appeal fails, escalate to government oversight.

State Insurance Department Complaint

Every state has an Insurance Commissioner or Department of Insurance. File a formal complaint online or by mail. Provide:

  • Claim number and policy number
  • Copy of the denial letter
  • Summary of why the denial is wrong
  • Your appeal and insurer's response
  • Contact information

The department investigates free of charge. Complaints are often resolved within 30-60 days. Departments report complaint statistics to the National Association of Insurance Commissioners, which pressures insurers with poor complaint records.

Independent External Review (for health insurance)

If your denial involves health insurance, you may qualify for independent external review. A neutral party outside the insurance company reviews your case. These often overturn denials, especially for treatment denials.

When to Hire an Attorney

Legal help can mean the difference between denial and recovery.

Hire an Attorney If...

  • The claim value exceeds $10,000
  • The insurer has denied a legitimate claim
  • Policy language is ambiguous
  • Your appeal was denied despite strong evidence
  • The insurer is acting in bad faith
  • You've exhausted internal appeals with no resolution
  • You face bad faith or unfair claim settlement practices

What Attorneys Can Do

  • Review policy language and identify coverage you have
  • Conduct legal research on state law and case precedent
  • Hire expert witnesses (engineers, doctors) to support your case
  • Negotiate directly with insurer (often more effective)
  • File bad faith lawsuits if warranted
  • Recover attorney fees in bad faith cases (insurer pays)

Many attorneys work on contingency or hourly rates. Some handle small cases on hourly billing ($200-$400/hour). For large claims, contingency (typically 33% of recovery) is common.

Pro tip: Send a demand letter from your attorney before filing suit. Insurers often settle quickly once they know legal costs will follow. This alone can overturn denials and recover attorney fees.

Bad Faith Claims

If an insurer denies a valid, clearly covered claim unreasonably, this may constitute bad faith. Some states allow you to sue for bad faith and recover punitive damages (extra damages to punish the insurer).

Bad Faith Indicators

  • Denial without investigating the claim
  • Misrepresenting policy language
  • Ignoring evidence supporting your claim
  • Refusing to communicate or respond
  • Arbitrary or capricious denial
  • Pattern of wrongful denials

Bad faith cases are serious and can result in six or seven-figure awards. However, they require legal representation and are complex. Consult an attorney if you suspect bad faith.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to appeal?

Most states allow 30-60 days from the denial date. Check your denial letter for the exact deadline. Missing it usually waives your right to appeal internally, though you may still file a complaint with the insurance department.

Can I sue my insurance company?

Yes, you can sue for wrongful denial. However, you must first exhaust internal appeal procedures in most states. If the insurer denies despite clear coverage, you have grounds for bad faith lawsuit. Consult an attorney.

Will appealing hurt my chances?

No. Appealing a claim doesn't increase future premiums or reduce future coverage. Insurers cannot retaliate for good faith appeals.

What if I provided incorrect information on my application?

If unintentional, argue honest mistake. Many states limit rescission to misstatements that would have changed the underwriting decision. Provide evidence of the true facts (prior applications, previous insurers, etc.).

How much will an attorney cost?

For large claims, contingency fees (33% of recovery) are standard. For smaller claims, hourly rates apply ($150-$500/hour depending on location and experience). Some firms offer flat fees for simple appeals.
