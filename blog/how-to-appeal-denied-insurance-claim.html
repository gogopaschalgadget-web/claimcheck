How to Appeal a Denied Insurance Claim: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

ClaimCheck | April 2026 | 11 min read

Your insurance company denied your claim. You are angry. Maybe you feel helpless. Before you accept the denial and pay out of pocket, understand this: between 40 and 60 percent of insurance appeals are decided in favor of the patient or claimant. That means nearly half of all denials can be reversed if you know what you are doing.

The problem is that almost no one appeals. Less than 1 percent of people who receive a denial letter actually go through the appeal process, according to data from Kaiser Family Foundation's 2024 analysis of insurer denial rates. Insurance companies count on this. A denial is not a final answer. It is the beginning of a negotiation.

This guide walks you through exactly what to do, in order, starting the moment you receive a denial.

The most important deadline you need to know: For health insurance claims, you must file your internal appeal within 180 days (6 months) of receiving the denial notice. Missing this window eliminates your right to appeal. Mark this date the moment you open a denial letter.

Understanding Why Claims Get Denied

Before you can appeal effectively, you need to know why you were denied. The most common denial reasons fall into four categories:

Coverage exclusions: The insurer says the service, procedure, or item is not covered under your plan. This is the most common denial type and also the most commonly overturned on appeal when you can show the service is medically necessary.

Medical necessity: The insurer agrees the service is generally covered but says it was not medically necessary in your case. This is the second most common denial, and it is typically fought with documentation from your doctor.

Pre-authorization failures: The service required prior authorization and either your provider did not get it, or the insurer claims they did not receive it. These are often resolved at the administrative level before a formal appeal.

Coding errors: A billing code was wrong or the claim form had a technical error. These denials are reversed through correction, not appeal. Call your provider first when you suspect this.

Step-by-Step: How to Appeal a Denied Insurance Claim

1 Read the Denial Letter Word for Word

The denial letter must tell you why you were denied and what your appeal rights are. Federal law requires this for health insurance plans. Read it carefully and note the specific reason code or language used. This exact language will be your target in the appeal. The letter will also state your appeal deadlines, the address where appeals are sent, and whether you can appeal by phone, in writing, or both. Always appeal in writing so you have a record.

2 Gather Your Documentation Before You Write Anything

Your appeal will succeed or fail based on the evidence you provide. Collect all of the following before drafting a single word of your appeal letter:

  • Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from the insurer
  • The original denial letter with the reason code
  • Your Summary of Benefits and Coverage (the document that describes what your plan covers)
  • All bills and receipts related to the denied claim
  • Medical records relevant to the denied service
  • A letter from your treating physician supporting the medical necessity of the service
  • Any clinical guidelines or peer-reviewed research supporting the treatment

The physician letter is the single most important document in a medical necessity appeal. Ask your doctor specifically to state that the treatment was medically necessary, that alternatives were considered and rejected, and what the likely outcome would be without the treatment.

3 File Your Internal Appeal

An internal appeal asks the insurance company to review its own decision. This sounds like asking the fox to guard the henhouse, and in some cases it is. But internal appeals reverse decisions roughly 40 percent of the time for health claims, and filing one is legally required before you can access external review.

Write a clear, factual appeal letter. State the claim number, the date of service, the denial reason, and why the denial is incorrect. Attach all supporting documentation. Keep copies of everything you send. Send via certified mail with return receipt if you are mailing a paper appeal. For online portals, screenshot the submission confirmation page.

The insurer must respond to your internal appeal within 30 days for non-urgent matters, or 72 hours for urgent medical situations, under federal law.

4 If Internal Appeal Fails: Request External Review

If your internal appeal is denied, you have the right to an independent external review. In an external review, a neutral third-party organization (not the insurer) reviews your claim and makes a binding decision. The insurance company cannot ignore or overturn an external reviewer's decision in your favor.

External review requests must be filed within 4 months of your final internal appeal denial for most plans. Contact your state insurance commissioner's office or, for federally regulated plans, the Department of Labor, to initiate the external review process. There is no cost to you to file an external review request.

External reviews favor claimants at a slightly lower rate than internal appeals, but when they do, the decision is final and binding on the insurer.

5 Know When to Escalate Further

If external review still goes against you, you still have options. You can file a complaint with your state insurance commissioner. You can contact a patient advocate or consumer assistance program. For denials over significant amounts, consulting an insurance attorney is worth considering. Many work on contingency for large claims, meaning they take a percentage of what they recover rather than charging you upfront.

Your state insurance commissioner's website will show you the complaint process. Many states have consumer assistance programs staffed by advocates who help you navigate appeals at no charge.

What Actually Works: Evidence From Real Appeals

Analysis of appealed health insurance claims shows consistent patterns in what reverses denials. Claims supported by a physician letter describing specific medical necessity are overturned at twice the rate of claims with only the original bill. Claims where the claimant cited specific plan language from their Summary of Benefits are overturned more often than claims that make general arguments about fairness.

53% of external appeals reviewed by independent reviewers were decided in favor of the claimant in 2024, according to CMS data.

The most common reason strong appeals still fail: missing the deadline. Set a calendar reminder the day you receive your denial letter. The 180-day internal appeal window and the 4-month external review window are hard cutoffs.

Auto Insurance and Property Claims: Same Process, Different Rules

The internal/external appeal structure applies primarily to health insurance. For auto and property claims, the process is similar but the names differ. You file a complaint or dispute with the insurer, request a re-inspection or independent appraisal, and can escalate to your state insurance commissioner. Most states also allow you to invoke the appraisal clause in your homeowner or auto policy, which lets you hire an independent appraiser to dispute the insurer's valuation.
